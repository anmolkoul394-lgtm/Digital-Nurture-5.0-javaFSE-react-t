# 1. Introduction to Agile & the Agile Manifesto

## Overview of Agile
Agile is an iterative approach to software delivery — build in small
increments, get feedback fast, adapt the plan continuously instead of
locking everything down upfront.

## The Agile Manifesto — 4 Values
1. **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools
2. **Working software** over comprehensive documentation
3. **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation
4. **Responding to change** over following a plan

*(Note: the items on the right still have value — Agile just values the items on the left more.)*

## The 12 Principles (summarized)
1. Highest priority: satisfy the customer through early, continuous delivery
2. Welcome changing requirements, even late in development
3. Deliver working software frequently (weeks, not months)
4. Business people and developers work together daily
5. Build projects around motivated individuals; trust them
6. Face-to-face conversation is the most efficient communication
7. Working software is the primary measure of progress
8. Sustainable pace — teams should be able to maintain it indefinitely
9. Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design
10. Simplicity — maximize the amount of work NOT done
11. Best architectures/requirements/designs emerge from self-organizing teams
12. Team reflects regularly and adjusts its behavior (retrospectives)

## Agile vs Waterfall
| | Waterfall | Agile |
|---|---|---|
| Approach | Sequential phases (requirements → design → build → test → release) | Iterative cycles (sprints) |
| Requirements | Fixed upfront | Evolve throughout the project |
| Customer feedback | Mostly at the end | Every sprint |
| Change | Expensive, disruptive late in the project | Expected and welcomed |
| Delivery | One big release | Frequent small releases |
| Best for | Well-understood, fixed-scope projects | Projects where requirements evolve |
